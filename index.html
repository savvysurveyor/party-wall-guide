Section 01

Understanding the Notice Types

The Act requires formal written notice before any notifiable work begins. The type of notice depends on what you're planning to do.

01

Party Structure Notice

For work directly to an existing party wall or structure shared with your neighbour. This includes:

  • Cutting into a party wall for beam insertion
  • Raising a party wall or party fence wall
  • Demolishing and rebuilding a party wall
  • Underpinning a party wall
  • Cutting away projections (e.g. chimney breasts)
⏰ Minimum 2 months before starting works

Adjacent Excavation Notice

Required when excavating near a neighbouring building's foundations. There are two trigger distances:

  • Within 3 metres: Any excavation lower than the neighbour's foundations (Section 6(1))
  • Within 6 metres: Excavation that would cut a 45-degree line drawn from the bottom of the neighbour's foundations (Section 6(2))
⏰ Minimum 1 month before starting works

Line of Junction Notice

For building a new wall at or astride the boundary line with your neighbour. This covers:

  • New walls built up to the boundary line
  • New walls built astride the boundary (requires neighbour's written consent)
  • Typically arises with extensions or new-build boundary walls
⏰ Minimum 1 month before starting works

Section 02

Building Owner Process

The step-by-step process for building owners planning notifiable works — from initial notice through to completion.

02
01

Determine if Your Works are Notifiable

Before you begin

Not all building work requires party wall notification. Check whether your planned works trigger the Act.

Works that typically require notice
  • Loft conversions involving a party wall
  • Extensions within 3 or 6 metres of a neighbour's foundations
  • Inserting a steel beam into a party wall
  • Removing a chimney breast from a party wall
  • Underpinning shared foundations
Works that usually don't require notice
  • Internal alterations not affecting the party wall
  • Drilling into a party wall (minor fixings)
  • Plastering or decorating a party wall surface

Tip: If in doubt, instruct a party wall surveyor for preliminary advice. Getting this wrong can delay your project significantly.

Read more ▶
02

Instruct a Party Wall Surveyor

Recommended: 2–3 months before works

Engage a qualified surveyor early. They'll confirm which notices are required and prepare them correctly.

What a surveyor provides
  • Assessment of which notice types apply
  • Preparation and serving of formal notices
  • Guidance on timescales and obligations
  • Schedule of Condition of adjoining properties
  • Party Wall Award if consent isn't given

Important: The Building Owner is responsible for the reasonable costs of the Adjoining Owner's surveyor — this is a statutory obligation under Section 10(13) of the Act.

Read more ▶
03

Serve the Party Wall Notice

2 months (S.1) or 1 month (S.2/S.6) before works

Formal written notice must be served on every affected adjoining owner. The notice must contain specific prescribed information.

Notice must include
  • Your name and address (the Building Owner)
  • The nature and particulars of the proposed works
  • The date when works are proposed to begin
  • Reference to the relevant section of the Act
Methods of service
  • Hand delivery (recommended — retain proof)
  • Recorded delivery post
  • Left at the last known address

A notice remains valid for 12 months from its stated commencement date. After that, a fresh notice is required.

Read more ▶
04

Wait for the Adjoining Owner's Response

14-day response period

The Adjoining Owner has 14 days from receiving the notice to respond. There are three possible outcomes.

Possible responses
  • Consent: Works can proceed without an Award. Record the consent in writing.
  • Dissent: A dispute is deemed to have arisen. Surveyors must be appointed to resolve it via a Party Wall Award.
  • No response: After 14 days, a dispute is deemed to have arisen by default. The process continues as if they dissented.

The Building Owner cannot start notifiable works until either consent is received in writing or a Party Wall Award has been made and served.

Read more ▶
05

Party Wall Award & Schedule of Condition

Typically 4–8 weeks

If a dispute arises, surveyors prepare a Party Wall Award that governs how the works must be carried out.

The Award typically includes
  • Detailed description of the permitted works
  • Schedule of Condition — photographic record of the adjoining property before works begin
  • Working hours and access arrangements
  • Requirements for protection and making good
  • Costs allocation (usually borne by the Building Owner)

Either party has 14 days from service of the Award to appeal to the County Court. If no appeal is made, the Award is binding.

Read more ▶
06

Commence Works & Comply with the Award

Duration of construction

Once consent is received or an Award has been served (and the appeal period has passed), works can begin.

Obligations during works
  • Comply strictly with the terms of the Award
  • Minimise inconvenience to the Adjoining Owner
  • Allow the Adjoining Owner's surveyor reasonable access to inspect
  • Make good any damage caused by the works (Section 7(2))
  • Maintain adequate insurance

If damage occurs to the adjoining property, the Building Owner is liable to make good unless the Award states otherwise.

Section 03

Response Outcomes

What happens after a notice is served depends on how the Adjoining Owner responds within the 14-day window.

03
✓

Consent Given

The Adjoining Owner agrees to the proposed works in writing. No Award is needed. Works may proceed after the statutory notice period expires. Record the written consent carefully.

→ Works proceed — no Award required
✗

Dissent (Dispute)

The Adjoining Owner disagrees or wants safeguards. A dispute is deemed to have arisen. Each party appoints a surveyor (or agrees on a single Agreed Surveyor). The surveyors prepare a Party Wall Award.

→ Surveyors appointed — Award prepared
?

No Response (14 days)

If no response is received within 14 days, a dispute is deemed to have arisen automatically. The Building Owner may appoint a surveyor on behalf of the Adjoining Owner (Section 10(4)(b)).

→ Deemed dispute — surveyor appointed
Section 04

Adjoining Owner Process

If you've received a party wall notice, here's what you need to know and do — your rights are protected by the Act.

04
01

Receive and Review the Notice

Day 0 — notice received

Check the notice carefully. It should identify the Building Owner, describe the proposed works, reference the relevant section of the Act, and state a proposed start date.

What to check
  • Is the notice properly addressed to you?
  • Does it describe the works clearly?
  • Is it referencing the correct section (1, 2, or 6)?
  • Is the proposed start date realistic given the notice period?

If anything is unclear or seems incorrect, seek advice from a party wall surveyor before responding.

Read more ▶
02

Decide: Consent or Dissent

Within 14 days

You have 14 days to respond. You can give written consent (works proceed without an Award) or dissent (triggering the surveyor appointment process).

Reasons you might consent
  • The works are minor and won't affect your property
  • You trust the Building Owner and their contractor
  • You want to maintain a good neighbourly relationship
Reasons you might dissent
  • You want a formal Schedule of Condition for protection
  • The works are significant and could affect your property
  • You want enforceable terms governing working hours, access, and making good
  • You want professional oversight of the works

Important: Dissenting does NOT prevent the works from going ahead. It simply ensures proper legal safeguards are in place.

Read more ▶
03

Appoint Your Surveyor

Within 10 days of dissent

If you dissent, you should appoint a surveyor within 10 days. If you don't, the Building Owner may appoint one on your behalf under Section 10(4)(b).

Your surveyor's role
  • Acts impartially to resolve the dispute (not as your advocate)
  • Inspects your property and prepares a Schedule of Condition
  • Negotiates the terms of the Party Wall Award
  • Ensures your property is protected during the works

Costs: The Building Owner is generally responsible for the reasonable fees of the Adjoining Owner's surveyor. You should not normally need to pay anything.

Read more ▶
04

Award Served & Right of Appeal

14-day appeal window

Once the surveyors agree the Award, it is served on both parties. You have 14 days to appeal to the County Court if you disagree with any of its terms.

What the Award provides
  • Legal framework governing how works are carried out
  • Schedule of Condition of your property
  • Provisions for making good any damage
  • Working hours and access restrictions
  • Obligations on the Building Owner

If damage occurs during the works, the Schedule of Condition provides the baseline evidence for any claim.

Section 05

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers to the most common questions about the Party Wall etc. Act 1996, the process, costs, and your rights.

05
A party wall is a wall that stands on the land of two or more owners and forms part of a building. It can also be a wall that stands on one owner's land but is used by two or more owners to separate their buildings (a party fence wall). The Party Wall etc. Act 1996 provides a framework for preventing and resolving disputes relating to party walls, boundary walls, and excavations near neighbouring buildings.
If your extension involves work to a party wall (e.g. cutting into it for a beam) or excavation within 3 or 6 metres of a neighbouring building's foundations, you will likely need to serve a party wall notice. Even if the extension doesn't touch the party wall directly, foundation excavation close to the boundary can trigger Section 6 of the Act. It's best to consult a party wall surveyor early in your project planning.
The Building Owner (the person carrying out the works) is generally responsible for the reasonable costs of the entire process, including the Adjoining Owner's surveyor's fees. This is a statutory obligation under Section 10(13) of the Act. The Adjoining Owner should not normally need to pay anything unless they are also carrying out notifiable works.
No. The Party Wall etc. Act 1996 exists to facilitate works, not prevent them. Your neighbour cannot refuse your works — even if they dissent, the dispute resolution process through appointed surveyors will produce an Award that allows your works to proceed under agreed terms. However, you cannot start notifiable works until either consent is given or an Award has been served.
Starting notifiable works without serving proper notice is a breach of the Act. Your neighbour can seek an injunction through the County Court to stop your works immediately. This can be extremely costly and cause significant delays to your project. The court may also award damages. Always ensure proper notices are served and either consent is obtained or an Award is in place before commencing works.
A Schedule of Condition is a detailed photographic and written record of the current state of the adjoining property before works begin. It records any existing cracks, defects, or damage so that if any new damage occurs during the works, there is a clear baseline for comparison. It forms part of the Party Wall Award and is a critical piece of evidence if a damage claim arises.
If the Adjoining Owner consents in writing, the process is complete as soon as the statutory notice period expires (2 months for Section 1, 1 month for Sections 2 and 6). If a dispute arises and an Award is needed, the process typically takes 4–8 weeks from surveyor appointment, though complex cases can take longer. Factor this into your project programme — ideally begin the party wall process 3 months before your intended start date.
Yes. Both parties can agree to appoint a single "Agreed Surveyor" under Section 10(1)(a) of the Act. This is often quicker and more cost-effective. The Agreed Surveyor must act impartially — they do not represent either party. However, either party can at any time appoint their own surveyor instead, which converts the process to a two-surveyor arrangement.
If the adjoining property is unoccupied or the owner cannot be traced, the notice can be served by fixing it to the property and sending a copy by registered post to the last known address. If no response is received within 14 days, a dispute is deemed to have arisen and you may proceed to appoint a surveyor on their behalf under Section 10(4)(b). A Land Registry title search can help identify the current owner.
The Act covers "party fence walls" — walls that stand on the boundary but do not form part of a building. However, garden fences (timber) are not covered by the Party Wall etc. Act 1996. Boundary disputes about fences are a separate matter, typically governed by common law and the Land Registry. If you're building a new brick wall on or near the boundary, the Act may well apply.
Section 8 of the Act grants the Building Owner and their workpeople the right to enter the Adjoining Owner's property to carry out works authorised by the Award, provided at least 14 days' written notice is given (or 1 day in case of emergency). This right of access is a statutory provision — it cannot be unreasonably refused. However, the Building Owner must cause as little inconvenience as possible and must compensate for any loss or damage.
Yes. Either party has 14 days from the date of service of the Award to appeal to the County Court under Section 10(17). The appeal is a complete rehearing — the court will consider the matter afresh, not just review the surveyors' decision. Appeals are relatively rare and can be costly, but they provide an important safeguard if you believe the Award is fundamentally unfair or incorrect.
The party wall process is completely separate from the planning process. You may need both, but they run independently. Planning permission deals with whether you can build; the Party Wall Act deals with how the works affect your neighbour's property. Neither process depends on the other, though both should be addressed early in your project planning to avoid delays.
